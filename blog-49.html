Why File Size Limits Exist — and Why They're Frustrating

Banks, universities, HR portals, and government websites frequently enforce file size limits of 100KB, 200KB, or 500KB for uploaded documents. These limits are often set for server storage, older system compatibility, or simply outdated policies that haven't been updated in years.

The frustrating part: a simple 5-page scanned form might be 3MB just because the scanner saved it at 300 DPI with colour information. The actual text content would fit in a fraction of that size.

What Makes a PDF Large?

  • Embedded high-resolution images: A single photo scanned at 300 DPI can be 1–5MB on its own.
  • Embedded fonts: PDFs sometimes store entire font files internally — up to several hundred KB each.
  • Metadata and hidden layers: Comments, revision history, editing layers, and invisible objects add invisible bulk.
  • Uncompressed content streams: Older PDF creators don't apply compression, leaving raw data in the file.
  • Multiple scanned pages: Every scanned page is essentially a photo — 5 pages at 600 DPI can easily exceed 10MB.
⚠️ Important: What Gets Affected by Compression

Heavy compression reduces image quality inside the PDF. For documents that contain only text (like contracts, letters, or typed forms), compression is nearly lossless. For scanned documents with photos or fine detail, expect some visual reduction at very small target sizes.

Pro Tips for Getting Under 100KB

Tip 1: Remove Unnecessary Pages First

Use PDFSnap's Split PDF or Delete Pages tool to remove blank pages, cover sheets, or appendices before compressing. Fewer pages always means a smaller file.

Tip 2: Convert to Grayscale

Colour information triples the data needed for each pixel. If the document doesn't need colour (most text documents don't), converting to grayscale before or during compression can dramatically reduce size.

Tip 3: Check for Embedded Fonts

If you created the PDF from a Word or PowerPoint document, try re-saving with font embedding disabled, then convert again. Many common fonts (Arial, Times New Roman) don't need to be embedded.

Tip 4: Use 72–96 DPI for Screen-Only Documents

If the compressed PDF will only ever be viewed on screen (not printed), downsampling images to 72–96 DPI is invisible to readers but cuts file size by 60–80%.

Tip 5: Try Multiple Passes

If the first compression doesn't reach your target, compress the output again. A second pass often squeezes out another 20–30% — though diminishing returns apply after the third pass.

Frequently Asked Questions

❓ Can every PDF be compressed to 100KB?
Not always. A text-only 2-page PDF might already be under 100KB. A 50-page colour-scanned document may only compress to 500KB before quality becomes unacceptable. For most 1–5 page scanned forms, reaching 100KB is achievable with maximum compression settings.
❓ Will the text remain readable after compression?
Yes — text in PDFs is stored as vector data, not pixels, so it remains perfectly sharp regardless of compression level. Only embedded images lose quality during aggressive compression.
❓ Is it safe to compress sensitive documents online?
With PDFSnap, yes. All compression happens in your browser — your file is never uploaded to a server. You can even turn off your Wi-Fi after loading the tool and it still works, proving the processing is entirely local.
❓ What's the maximum file size I can compress?
PDFSnap has no artificial file size limit. Practical limits depend on your device RAM. Files up to 100MB work fine on most modern phones and computers. Very large files (500MB+) may slow down on older devices.
❓ Why is my compressed PDF still too large?
The file is likely dominated by high-resolution scanned images. Try: (1) deleting unnecessary pages first, (2) using the maximum compression setting, (3) converting to grayscale before compressing. A combination of all three usually gets most documents under 100KB.
