PNG vs JPG — What's Actually Different

Understanding the core difference helps you make the right decision every time — not just follow generic advice.

🔵 PNG Lossless

  • No quality loss when saved
  • Supports transparency (clear backgrounds)
  • Larger file sizes — especially for photos
  • Best for logos, icons, screenshots, graphics with text
  • Every pixel stored exactly as-is
  • Can be saved and re-saved with zero degradation

🟠 JPG Lossy

  • Slight quality loss — invisible at high quality settings
  • No transparency support (white background fills in)
  • Much smaller file sizes — ideal for photos
  • Best for photographs, social media images, web images
  • Uses smart compression that matches how human eyes work
  • Re-saving multiple times accumulates quality loss
💡 The One Rule to Remember

If your image has a transparent background — keep it as PNG. Converting to JPG will fill that transparent area with a solid white (or black) background, destroying the transparency permanently. For everything else — photos, screenshots, social media images — JPG is almost always the better choice.

When to Convert PNG to JPG — And When NOT To

✅ Convert PNG → JPG When:

  • It's a photograph — photos have millions of color variations that JPG handles perfectly with a fraction of the file size
  • You need to upload to social media — Instagram, Facebook, and Twitter all prefer JPG for photos
  • Email attachment is too large — a 5MB PNG photo can become a 500KB JPG with no visible quality difference
  • Website performance matters — smaller JPGs load faster, improve SEO, and reduce bandwidth costs
  • A platform only accepts JPG — many portals, forms, and apps specify JPG only
  • You're storing thousands of photos — JPG takes up dramatically less storage space

❌ Do NOT Convert PNG → JPG When:

  • The image has a transparent background — logos, icons, stickers, cutout images. JPG will add a white background.
  • It contains text or sharp lines — JPG compression creates visible artifacts around sharp edges and text. Screenshots, infographics, and diagrams look worse as JPG.
  • It's a logo you'll resize — keep logos as PNG (or SVG) so they stay sharp at any size
  • You'll edit it further — each JPG save adds compression artifacts. Keep working files as PNG, only export as JPG for final delivery
⚠️ Never Convert a JPG Back to PNG and Call It "Better Quality"

Once a JPG has been compressed, the lost data is gone forever. Converting that JPG to PNG gives you a larger file but does NOT restore quality — it just wraps the same degraded pixels in a lossless container. The only way to get true PNG quality is to start from the original uncompressed source.

JPG to PNG — The Reverse Conversion

Sometimes you need to go the other direction — converting JPG to PNG. The most common reasons:

  • You need to add a transparent background — a design tool needs a PNG to remove the background from a product photo
  • Platform requires PNG — some tools, apps, and design platforms specifically require PNG input
  • You'll be editing heavily — converting to PNG before multiple rounds of editing prevents JPG compression from stacking up
  • Combining with other PNGs — keeping all assets in the same format makes design work easier

PDFSnap's JPG to PNG tool works the same way — browser-based, instant, free, no upload. Find it in the Image Tools section on the homepage.

📌 Remember: JPG → PNG Does NOT Add Transparency

Converting a JPG to PNG does not magically give it a transparent background. The white or colored background from the JPG stays — it's just now stored in a lossless PNG container. To remove backgrounds you need a background removal tool, not just a format converter.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does converting PNG to JPG lose quality?
At High quality settings, the difference is invisible to the human eye for photographs. JPG uses a compression algorithm that discards image data that human vision is least sensitive to. You'd need to zoom in to 300%+ and compare side by side to see any difference. For text, sharp lines, and logos however, JPG compression artifacts can be visible — stick with PNG for those.
What happens to transparent areas when I convert PNG to JPG?
Transparent pixels are filled with a solid background color — usually white. JPG does not support transparency at all, so this is unavoidable. If your image has a transparent background that you need to preserve, do not convert it to JPG. Keep it as PNG or consider WebP which supports both transparency and better compression.
Is PNG to JPG conversion reversible?
Not truly. Converting JPG back to PNG gives you a larger file but does not restore the quality lost during JPG compression. The original compressed data cannot be recovered. Always keep your original PNG files before converting — PDFSnap downloads the converted file separately, so your original is never modified.
Can I convert multiple PNG files at once?
The current tool converts one image at a time for full control over each conversion. Each conversion takes only 2–3 seconds, so processing a batch of images is still very quick.
Which quality setting should I use?
For most purposes — social media, email, web — Medium quality gives the best balance of file size and visual quality. Use High quality for print-ready images or professional photography. Use Low quality only for thumbnails, previews, or situations where file size is the absolute priority.
Does PDFSnap upload my images to a server?
Never. PDFSnap's converter uses the HTML5 Canvas API inside your browser to perform the conversion entirely on your device. Your images are never transmitted to any server. You can verify this by opening your browser's network inspector — you'll see zero image upload requests when using the tool.
